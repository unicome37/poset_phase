\begin{abstract}
We study dimensional selection in finite ensembles of Lorentzian-like
causal sets sprinkled into flat spacetimes of dimension two through five.
A link-based causal action exhibits a coupling window
in which four-dimensional families consistently outscore all others,
whereas the standard four-dimensional Benincasa--Dowker--Glaser action
systematically selects five dimensions at every coupling tested.
Component analysis traces this failure to the higher-order interval
corrections, which preferentially favor causally sparse,
high-dimensional configurations.
The present work concerns finite causal-set ensembles rather than
a controlled continuum limit.

We introduce a dimensionless diagnostic that measures
the link-penalty separation per unit entropy separation between
adjacent-dimensional families.
This ratio is approximately ten at the four-to-five boundary,
stable across three generator geometries and seven system sizes
up to 112 elements, and substantially larger than the corresponding
lower-dimensional thresholds.
A geometrically motivated semi-analytic derivation from Minkowski
light-cone geometry reproduces the numerical median to within
one per cent.
Blind extrapolation to six and seven dimensions yields qualitative
agreement with independent numerical measurements, supporting the
picture that inter-dimensional barriers intensify beyond four dimensions.
The resulting asymmetric barrier structure makes four-dimensionality
a favored regime of the entropy--connectivity competition:
it is not four dimensions itself that is special, but the barrier above it.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} causal set theory, dimensional selection,
Benincasa--Dowker action, discrete spacetime, Lorentzian posets,
quantum gravity, combinatorial entropy, linear extensions

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

\subsection{The Dimensionality Problem}
\label{sec:dim_problem}

Why observed macroscopic spacetime has $3{+}1$ effective dimensions remains one of the deepest open
problems in fundamental physics.
In many approaches --- string theory, Kaluza--Klein, large extra dimensions --- the answer is displaced
rather than derived: extra dimensions are postulated and later compactified or hidden.
A more fundamental question is:

\begin{quote}
Can a $3{+}1$-dimensional Lorentzian-like phase emerge as the structurally favored candidate inside a
broader discrete causal space, without being inserted by hand?
\end{quote}

\subsection{Causal Set Theory and the BDG Action}
\label{sec:causal_set}

Causal set theory \cite{Bombelli1987,Surya2019} provides one of the most natural frameworks for this
question. A causal set is a locally finite partially ordered set whose order relation encodes the causal
structure of spacetime.
The idea of probing the effective dimensionality of discrete spacetime has a long history;
the spectral-dimension approach \cite{Ambjorn2005} in causal dynamical triangulations
first demonstrated that a dynamically generated spacetime can exhibit
a running dimensionality, motivating complementary studies within the causal-set programme.
The Benincasa--Dowker--Glaser (BDG) action \cite{Benincasa2010} provides a
discrete analogue of the Einstein--Hilbert action:
\begin{equation}
S^{(d)}_\text{BDG} = \sum_{k=0}^{d/2} \alpha_k^{(d)} \, C_k
\label{eq:bdg_general}
\end{equation}
where $C_k$ counts the number of order-$k$ causal intervals (pairs of elements with exactly $k$ elements
between them), and $\alpha_k^{(d)}$ are dimension-dependent coefficients derived from continuum matching.
For $d=2$:
\begin{equation}
S^{(2)}_\text{BDG} = S_\text{link} = N - 2C_0
\label{eq:link_action}
\end{equation}
which we call the \emph{link action}: it counts only elements and direct links (covering relations).
For $d=4$:
\begin{equation}
S^{(4)}_\text{BDG} = N - C_0 + 9C_1 - 16C_2 + 8C_3
\label{eq:bdg_d4}
\end{equation}
with additional higher-order interval terms designed to recover the scalar curvature in the continuum limit \cite{Glaser2018}.
The extension to arbitrary even dimensions was established by Dowker and Glaser \cite{Dowker2013};
Aslanbeigi \emph{et al.}\ \cite{Aslanbeigi2014} subsequently showed how to generalize
this construction to all dimensions, providing the theoretical basis for the
coefficients used in the present study.

Most existing work on the BDG action focuses on the continuum limit, where the
higher-order coefficients are essential for recovering the Einstein--Hilbert action
and ensuring locality.  Far less attention has been given to the \emph{pre-geometric}
regime of small, finite causal sets, where the question is not ``how does curvature
emerge?''  but rather ``which dimensionality is structurally preferred?''
The present work investigates this gap by comparing the link sector and the full
BDG action in finite-dimensional competition.

\subsection{Summary of Results}
\label{sec:summary_results}

We report five principal results, organized in order of logical dependence:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{R\arabic*}.]
\item \textbf{The link action favors 4D.}
  In a score function $\mathrm{Score}(G;\lambda) = -\beta \cdot H(G) + \lambda \cdot S(G)/N$,
  the link action produces a $\lambda$-window ($\lambda \approx 6{-}8$) where 4D families
  consistently outscore 2D, 3D, and 5D. The 4D selection is stable across $N = 20{-}68$
  and extends to $N = 112$, with the window shifting to higher $\lambda$ at $N \geq 96$
  (Sections~\ref{sec:link_action_results}--\ref{sec:large_n}).

\item \textbf{The standard BDG $d{=}4$ action favors 5D instead.}
  With literature coefficients, the BDG action systematically picks 5D at
  every coupling, for all sizes $N = 20{-}68$, and across all tested generators.
  Component analysis shows that the $C_1$--$C_3$ correction terms amplify
  the entropy advantage of 5D, destroying the link action's dimensional barrier
  (Section~\ref{sec:bdg_failure}).

\item \textbf{The $\Xi$ parameter captures the dominant boundary asymmetry.}
  We define a dimensionless control variable
  $\Xi_{d \to d+1} = |\Delta \bar S| / |\Delta \bar h|$
  measuring the link-action separation per unit entropy separation.
  $\Xi_{4\to5} \approx 10$ is stable across three generators (CV~$= 13.9\%$)
  and across sizes from $N = 20$ to $N = 112$ (CV~$= 17.7\%$)
  (Section~\ref{sec:xi_parameter}).

\item \textbf{Semi-analytic derivation: $\Xi_{4 \to 5} = 11.31$.}
  Starting from the Minkowski ordering fraction $p_d$ and the mean
  Alexandrov volume $\mathbb{E}[V_A]$, we derive a geometrically motivated
  entropy closure
  $H/N = (1-\pi/48)(1-p_d)(\ln N-1) + (\pi/24)\left(1-e^{-(N-2)\mathbb{E}[V_A]}\right)$.
  This yields $\Xi_{4\to5} = 11.31$, within $0.4\%$ of the numerical
  median $11.35$, with no additional fitted parameter once $\varepsilon = \pi/48$
  is imposed. The barrier is traced to Minkowski
  light-cone thinning plus interval-occupancy saturation
  (Section~\ref{sec:xi_derivation}).

\item \textbf{Qualitative out-of-sample support: $d \geq 6$ extrapolation.}
  Blind predictions $\Xi_{5\to6} = 64.4$ and $\Xi_{6\to7} = 51.3$
  are broadly consistent with numerical measurements at $45.8$ and $37.8$ respectively
  (overestimation $\sim 27\%$). Three qualitative features are reproduced:
  massive amplification, non-monotonic plateau, and correct order of magnitude
  (Section~\ref{sec:d6_validation}).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The Key Physics}
\label{sec:key_physics}

The dimensional barrier above 4D is \emph{asymmetrically steep}.
$\Xi_{4\to5}$ exceeds $\Xi_{3\to4}$ by an order of magnitude:
suppressing 5D requires far less coupling than suppressing 4D from below.
For any coupling strong enough to hold back 5D,
4D still beats 3D by entropic margin.
Within this framework, what singles out $3{+}1$ dimensions is not an intrinsic
optimality of 4D, but the anomalously high structural price of crossing
from 4D to 5D.

\subsection{Relation to Prior Work}
\label{sec:prior_work}

Carlip \cite{Carlip2024} reviewed the role of dimensionality in quantum gravity
and argued that suppression of non-manifold-like (Kleitman--Rothschild)
sets in the causal-set path integral comes primarily from the link term in the action,
with higher-order terms not critical for that problem.
Loomis and Carlip \cite{Loomis2018} established the detailed
non-manifold suppression mechanism. Mathur, Singh, and Surya \cite{Mathur2020} studied entropy
and the link action in more detail.
Surya, Nomaan~X, and Yazdi \cite{Surya2012} provided an early extensive
numerical study of the causal-set path integral and its dependence
on the BDG coupling, motivating the exploration of link-sector competition.
Brightwell and Georgiou \cite{Brightwell2010} and Dhar \cite{Dhar1978}
established key results on the enumeration and structure of linear extensions
of finite posets, which underpin the entropy estimation approach used here.

Our work extends this line by showing that the link term also controls \emph{dimensional} selection
among Lorentzian families, and that the standard $d{=}4$ BDG coefficients are counterproductive
for this specific question. The $\Xi$ parameter provides a quantitative framework for understanding
\emph{why} the dimensional boundary at 4D is special.

into a spacetime region and retaining the causal order.

\subsection{Entropy}
\label{sec:entropy}

Let $|L(G)|$ denote the number of linear extensions (total orders consistent with the
partial order) of a poset $G$. The entropy is
\begin{equation}
H(G) = \ln |L(G)|,
\label{eq:entropy_def}
\end{equation}
measured in nats (natural logarithm throughout this paper).
For small posets ($N \leq N_\text{exact}$), we compute $|L(G)|$ by dynamic programming.
For larger posets, we use the Sequential Importance Sampling (SIS)
algorithm~\cite{Cunningham2020,Huber2006} with 4096 runs.

\subsection{Interval Counts}
\label{sec:intervals}

For each poset, we compute the four interval counts $C_0, C_1, C_2, C_3$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $C_0$ (links): number of covering relations ($x \prec y$ with no element between them)
  \item $C_1$: pairs with exactly 1 element between them
  \item $C_2$: pairs with exactly 2 elements between them
  \item $C_3$: pairs with exactly 3 elements between them
\end{itemize}

Interval counts are computed by enumerating all comparable pairs $(x, y)$ in the transitive closure
of the partial order and classifying each by the cardinality of the open interval
$\{z : x \prec z \prec y\}$.
Links ($C_0$) correspond to covering relations in the Hasse diagram, i.e., pairs with
an empty open interval.

\subsection{Scoring Function}
\label{sec:scoring}

Each family $F$ is assigned a score at coupling $\lambda$:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Score}(G;\lambda) = -\beta \cdot H(G) + \lambda \cdot \frac{S(G)}{N}
\label{eq:score}
\end{equation}
where $S(G)$ is the causal action (link action or BDG), $\beta = 1$, and $\lambda$ controls
the relative weight of the dynamical term. At $\lambda = 0$, the highest-entropy family
wins; as $\lambda$ increases, the action term penalizes structurally unfavorable configurations.

\section{Action Variants}
\label{sec:action_variants}

\subsection{Link Action ($d{=}2$ BDG)}
\label{sec:link_action_def}

\begin{equation}
S_\text{link} = N - 2C_0
\end{equation}
This is the minimal BDG action. It penalizes posets with many links (dense causal structure)
and rewards those with few links (sparse structure). Its simplicity --- dependence on $C_0$
alone --- makes it the natural probe for questions about the density of covering relations.

\subsection{Standard BDG $d{=}4$}
\label{sec:bdg_d4_def}

\begin{equation}
S^{(4)} = N - C_0 + 9C_1 - 16C_2 + 8C_3
\end{equation}
The literature coefficients are designed for recovering the Einstein--Hilbert action in $d = 4$
Lorentzian manifolds. The alternating signs create partial cancellations between interval orders.
We do not interpret the failure of the standard $d{=}4$ BDG action at dimensional
selection (Section~\ref{sec:bdg_failure}) as a flaw of the continuum-matching construction;
rather, it indicates that finite dimensional selection and continuum curvature reconstruction
probe different sectors of the discrete dynamics.

\subsection{Pure BDG (No Link Term)}
\label{sec:pure_bdg_def}

\begin{equation}
S_\text{pure} = 9C_1 - 16C_2 + 8C_3
\end{equation}
Removing the link term isolates the higher-order contribution. This diagnostic tests whether
dimensional selection resides in $C_0$ or in $C_1$--$C_3$.

\subsection{Corrected $d{=}2$ Action}
\label{sec:corrected_d2_def}

\begin{equation}
S^{(2)}_\text{corr} = \frac{2}{N}\left(N - 2C_0\right)
\end{equation}
This is a simple rescaling of the link action (Eq.~\ref{eq:link_action}) by the factor~$2/N$.
Since this rescaling is monotone in $C_0$, it preserves the winner ordering at each $\lambda$
and tests whether density-normalization changes the qualitative behavior.

\subsection{4D Selection Window}
\label{sec:4d_window}

At fixed $N = 68$, the link action selects 4D for $\lambda \in [5, 10]$.
Outside this window:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\lambda < 5$: 5D wins (entropy dominates)
  \item $\lambda > 10$: 3D wins (link penalty over-penalizes the sparse 4D structure)
\end{itemize}
The finite selection window is a generic feature of the score function in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:score}: there is no a priori reason for any family to dominate
at all couplings.

\subsection{Robustness Across Generators}
\label{sec:generator_robustness}

To test whether the BDG failure is specific to our cube-sprinkle generator,
we repeat the comparison using three generator types:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Original cube sprinkle} (seed-controlled, $[0,1]^d$)
  \item \textbf{Independent-seed cube sprinkle} (different random seed per sample)
  \item \textbf{Causal diamond (Alexandrov set)} sprinkle (future light cone of origin intersected with past light cone of $(T,0,\ldots)$)
\end{enumerate}

All three generators yield the same qualitative pattern:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Link action: 4D wins at $\lambda \approx 6{-}8$ (original cube), $\lambda \approx 5{-}7$ (independent seed), $\lambda \approx 7{-}10$ (diamond)
  \item BDG $d{=}4$: 5D wins at all $\lambda$ for all generators
\end{itemize}

The parameter-space \emph{location} of the 4D window shifts, but the \emph{existence}
of a 4D window under the link action, and its \emph{absence} under BDG $d{=}4$,
are generator-invariant.
The generator dependence mainly affects the location of the selection window in
coupling space, whereas the qualitative contrast between link action and standard
$d{=}4$ BDG weights remains unchanged.

\subsection{The $\Xi$ Parameter: A Dimensionless Control Variable}
\label{sec:xi_parameter}

The generator dependence of $\lambda^*$ (the coupling where 4D first wins) motivates the
search for a \emph{generator-insensitive} quantity. We define:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\Xi_{d \to d+1}(N, G) \equiv \frac{|\overline{S}_{d+1} - \overline{S}_d|}{|\overline{h}_{d+1} - \overline{h}_d|}
}
\label{eq:xi_def}
\end{equation}
where $\overline{S}_d = S_\text{link}/N$ is the link-action density and
$\overline{h}_d = H_d / N$ is the entropy density,
both averaged over samples of family $d$ at size $N$ with generator $G$.

$\Xi_{d \to d+1}$ measures the \emph{link-penalty cost per unit of entropy gained}
in crossing the $d \to d{+}1$ dimensional boundary.
If $\Xi$ is large, a small coupling $\lambda$ suffices to overcome the entropy advantage
of $d{+}1$; if $\Xi$ is small, substantial coupling is needed.

In explicit form:

\begin{equation}
\Xi = \frac{\text{link-penalty gap per element}}{\text{entropy gap per element}}
    = \frac{|\Delta(S_\text{link}/N)|}{|\Delta(H / N)|}
\end{equation}

generator dependence.

\subsection{Why This Matters}
\label{sec:xi_physics}

Consider the score competition at some $\lambda$:
\begin{equation}
\Delta\mathrm{Score}_{d \to d+1}
  = \underbrace{-(\overline{h}_{d+1} - \overline{h}_d)}_{\text{entropy pull toward } d{+}1 \;(<0)}
  + \lambda \underbrace{(\overline{S}_{d+1} - \overline{S}_d)}_{\text{link-penalty push away (>0)}}
\label{eq:score_competition}
\end{equation}
The critical coupling where $d$ and $d{+}1$ are tied is
$\lambda^*_{d \to d+1} = 1 / \Xi_{d \to d+1}$.
Since $\Xi_{4 \to 5} \approx 10$ is large, very little coupling suffices to suppress 5D:
$\lambda^*_{4 \to 5} \approx 0.1$.
But $\Xi_{3 \to 4} \approx 3$ means $\lambda^*_{3 \to 4} \approx 0.3$,
so overcoming the entropy advantage of 4D over 3D requires substantially more coupling.

\paragraph{Note on scales.}
The threshold $\lambda^*_{d \to d+1} = 1/\Xi$ is the coupling at which
adjacent \emph{dimension-averaged} scores cross; it characterizes the
\emph{relative} boundary structure, not the absolute location of the 4D
selection window in the raw score convention of Eq.~\eqref{eq:score}.
The observed window ($\lambda \approx 5{-}10$ in Figure~\ref{fig:winner_heatmap})
arises from the \emph{joint} competition among all five families simultaneously,
and its position is further modulated by the log-base of $H$, the entropy
normalization, and sample-by-sample fluctuations.
What $\Xi$ captures is the dimensionless barrier \emph{asymmetry}
($\Xi_{4{\to}5} / \Xi_{3{\to}4} \gtrsim 3$), which is invariant
under these rescalings and determines the \emph{existence and width} of a
4D window, regardless of where that window falls in absolute $\lambda$.

This creates an asymmetric barrier:
\begin{equation}
\lambda^*_{4 \to 5} \ll \lambda^*_{3 \to 4}
\label{eq:asymmetric_barrier}
\end{equation}

For any $\lambda$ in the interval $(\lambda^*_{4 \to 5},\, \lambda^*_{3 \to 4})$,
5D is already suppressed but 4D is not yet undercut by 3D.
This is the 4D selection window --- and its width is controlled by the
\textbf{ratio $\Xi_{4 \to 5} / \Xi_{3 \to 4}$}, which is $\gtrsim 3$ across all generators.

The physical origin of the asymmetry:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Crossing from 4D to 5D, the entropy gain per element $\Delta(H/N)$ is modest
    ($\sim 0.15{-}0.29$), but the link-penalty relief is large ($\sim 1.0{-}2.9$).
    The system pays a steep ``price'' in structural sparsity for a small entropy reward.
  \item Crossing from 3D to 4D, the entropy gain is comparable ($\sim 0.22{-}0.43$),
    but the link-penalty relief is also comparable or smaller.
    The ratio is near unity; neither side dominates.
\end{itemize}

What stabilizes across generators is not $\lambda^*$ (which shifts),
not the absolute values of $\overline{S}_d$ or $\overline{h}_d$ (which depend on geometry),
but the \textbf{ratio $\Xi_{4 \to 5} \approx 10$} --- the intrinsic structural price of
entropy at the $4{\to}5$ boundary
(Figures~\ref{fig:xi_convergence} and~\ref{fig:xi_asymmetry}). This ratio transforms $\Xi_{4 \to 5}$ from a diagnostic into a candidate
generator-insensitive critical ratio of the link-density selection mechanism.

\subsection{Analytical Derivation of $\Xi_{4 \to 5} \approx 11$}
\label{sec:xi_derivation}

The numerical stability of $\Xi_{4 \to 5}$ motivates a geometrically motivated
semi-analytic derivation from the two geometric inputs of the cube-sprinkle ensemble:
the ordering fraction $p_d$ and the Alexandrov interval volume distribution.

\subsubsection{Geometric Inputs}
\label{sec:scaling_laws}

For two random points in the unit cube, let
\begin{equation}
p_d \equiv \mathbb{P}(|\Delta t| \geq \|\Delta \mathbf{x}\|)
\end{equation}
be the ordering fraction, and let
\begin{equation}
V_A = \kappa_d \tau^d,
\qquad
\kappa_d = \frac{\mathrm{Vol}(B_{d-1})}{2^{d-1}d}
\end{equation}
be the Alexandrov interval volume of a causal pair with proper time $\tau$.
Monte Carlo evaluation of the cube-sprinkle geometry gives
\begin{equation}
p_2 = 0.500, \quad p_3 = 0.291, \quad p_4 = 0.175, \quad p_5 = 0.105,
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
\kappa_2 = \frac{1}{2}, \quad
\kappa_3 = \frac{\pi}{12}, \quad
\kappa_4 = \frac{\pi}{24}, \quad
\kappa_5 = \frac{\pi^2}{160}.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Link Sector from Empty-Interval Geometry}
\label{sec:closed_form}

For a causal pair with interval volume $V_A$, the probability that no
intermediate element falls inside that interval is
\begin{equation}
P(\mathrm{link}\mid V_A) \approx (1-V_A)^{N-2}
\approx e^{-(N-2)V_A}.
\end{equation}
Hence the link density follows directly from Minkowski geometry:
\begin{equation}
\frac{C_0}{N}
\approx
\frac{N-1}{2}\,
\mathbb{E}\!\left[
\mathbf{1}_{\mathrm{causal}}(\Delta)\,
e^{-(N-2)V_A(\Delta)}
\right].
\label{eq:link_geom_density}
\end{equation}
This recovers the observed thinning of causal links with dimension without fitting
$C_0/N$ directly.

\subsubsection{Entropy Sector: Mean Field Plus Occupancy Restoration}
\label{sec:root_cause}

The mean-field entropy density is set by the incomparable-pair channel:
\begin{equation}
h_0(d,N) = (1-p_d)(\ln N - 1).
\end{equation}
This term alone underestimates the $4{\to}5$ barrier because it misses the
entropy carried by occupied causal intervals. The natural correction variable is
the probability that a causal interval contains at least one intermediate point:
\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{occ}} = 1 - \exp\!\bigl(-(N-2)\mathbb{E}[V_A]\bigr).
\label{eq:pocc}
\end{equation}
An explicit scan of the family $1-\exp(-\alpha (N-2)\mathbb{E}[V_A])$ over
$\alpha \in [0.3,2.5]$ shows that the optimum occurs at $\alpha \approx 1$;
the Boltzmann/Poisson occupancy form therefore requires no additional scale parameter.

Fitting the entropy as
\begin{equation}
\frac{H}{N} = B_0\,h_0 + B_1\,P_{\mathrm{occ}}
\end{equation}
reveals a non-trivial structural constraint:
\begin{equation}
B_1 + 2B_0 = 2.0001 \pm 0.025 \qquad (95\%~\mathrm{bootstrap~CI}),
\end{equation}
so that the two-parameter model collapses to the one-parameter form
\begin{equation}
\frac{H}{N}
=
h_0 - \varepsilon\,(h_0 - 2P_{\mathrm{occ}}),
\qquad
\varepsilon = 1-B_0.
\label{eq:entropy_transfer}
\end{equation}
The factor of $2$ has a direct combinatorial meaning: every occupied interval
involves two endpoints, and each endpoint acquires an entropy restitution of
$\varepsilon$ from the chain structure inside the interval.

Among simple analytic candidates, the best match is
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon \approx \frac{\kappa_4}{2} = \frac{\pi}{48} = 0.06545,
\end{equation}
which differs from the fitted value by only $0.42\%$.
This yields the parameter-free closure (once $\varepsilon = \pi/48$ is imposed)
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\frac{H}{N}
=
\left(1-\frac{\pi}{48}\right)(1-p_d)(\ln N - 1)
\;+\;
\frac{\pi}{24}\left(1-e^{-(N-2)\mathbb{E}[V_A]}\right)
}
\label{eq:entropy_zero_param}
\end{equation}
in which every quantity is fixed by Minkowski geometry.

\subsubsection{Prediction for $\Xi_{4\to5}$}
\label{sec:ordering_fraction}

Substituting Eq.~\eqref{eq:link_geom_density} and Eq.~\eqref{eq:entropy_zero_param}
into the definition of $\Xi_{d\to d+1}$ gives a geometrically reconstructed prediction.
For the seven tested sizes $N = 20{-}112$, the median prediction is
\begin{equation}
\Xi_{4\to5}^{\mathrm{geom}} = 11.31,
\end{equation}
to be compared with the numerical median
\begin{equation}
\Xi_{4\to5}^{\mathrm{num}} = 11.35.
\end{equation}
The relative error is only $0.4\%$, with no free fitted parameter once
$\varepsilon = \pi/48$ is imposed.
We do not claim uniqueness of this closure; rather, among simple analytic
candidates, the choice $\varepsilon = \pi/48$ provides a particularly accurate
geometric reconstruction of the observed $4{\to}5$ barrier.
Table~\ref{tab:xi_prediction} compares this zero-parameter closure against
alternative entropy closures.

\subsubsection{Why Occupancy Outperforms Exact Non-Link Fraction}
\label{sec:prediction_stability}

The most counterintuitive feature of the derivation is that the occupancy variable
$P_{\mathrm{occ}}$ predicts entropy better than the exact non-link fraction $1-\ell_d$.
This is a Jensen-gap effect. Because
\begin{equation}
\ell_d = \mathbb{E}\!\left[e^{-(N-2)V_A}\right],
\end{equation}
the coarse-grained occupancy approximation
\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{occ}} = 1 - e^{-(N-2)\mathbb{E}[V_A]}
\end{equation}
systematically overestimates $1-\ell_d$. The overestimate reaches $\sim 64\%$
for $d=4$, $N=112$, but only $\sim 41\%$ for $d=5$, producing an asymmetric
enhancement that compensates the residual mean-field bias on the entropy side.
Entropy therefore tracks a saturated occupancy variable rather than the raw
non-link fraction itself.

If the goal is instead to reconstruct $\ell_d$ itself, a Gamma-MGF closure
is more appropriate. Matching the mean and variance of
$X=(N-2)V_A$ gives
\begin{equation}
\ell_d^{\Gamma}
=
\left(1+\frac{\sigma^2}{m_1}\right)^{-m_1^2/\sigma^2},
\qquad
m_1 = \mathbb{E}[X].
\end{equation}
This recovers $\ell_d$ to better than $1\%$ in both $d=4$ and $d=5$, but it does
not improve $\Xi_{4\to5}$; the remaining issue is variable choice, not link-side
precision.

\label{sec:blind_predictions}

\end{equation}
the $\Xi$ ratio admits the closed form
\begin{equation}

\Xi_{d \to d+1}(N) =

\end{equation}

\boxed{

}
\label{eq:blind_predictions}
\end{equation}
Both values are dramatically larger than $\Xi_{4 \to 5} \approx 11$,
predicting that the dimensional barrier intensifies beyond $d = 5$.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{A Dual-Layer Picture}
\label{sec:dual_layer}

The numerical results are naturally interpreted as separating two
functional roles of the BDG action:

\paragraph{Layer~1 --- Dimensional selection (links).}
The density of Hasse covering relations controls which dimensionality is favored.
The link action $S = N - 2C_0$ captures the competition between entropic growth
(favoring higher $d$) and causal connectivity cost (penalizing higher $d$).
The optimum falls at $d = 4$.

\paragraph{Layer~2 --- Curvature and locality recovery (full BDG).}
The higher-order interval terms ($C_1, C_2, C_3$) are essential for reconstructing the
Einstein--Hilbert action in the continuum limit and for ensuring locality.
But these terms do not participate in --- and in fact destroy --- dimensional selection.

This dual-layer picture resolves what might otherwise seem like a tension:
the BDG action is designed to recover the Einstein--Hilbert action in the continuum
limit, yet it fails at dimensional selection.  The resolution is that the BDG
coefficients ($+9, -16, +8$ for $d{=}4$) are tuned to cancel non-local contributions
in the \emph{continuum} regime, where the interval spectrum is dense and dominated
by the universal short-distance behavior of the causal diamond volume.
In the \emph{finite, pre-geometric} regime probed here, the interval spectrum is far
from its continuum shape: higher-dimensional posets have extremely sparse
higher-order intervals ($C_1, C_2, C_3 \to 0$ as $d$ increases),
so the BDG correction terms --- calibrated for a dense 4D manifold --- over-correct
for the high-$d$ families and under-correct for low-$d$ families.
The $+9C_1$ term in particular imposes a massive penalty on 4D (which has abundant
order-1 intervals) while barely touching 5D (whose intervals are almost entirely links).
Thus the same coefficients that ensure correct curvature recovery at large $N$ in 4D
\emph{amplify} the entropy advantage of causally sparse high-dimensional configurations
at finite $N$.

\subsection{Physical Interpretation: Why 3+1?}
\label{sec:why_31}

The link action implements a simple physical principle:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Score} = -\text{(entropy)} + \lambda \cdot \text{(link action density)}
\end{equation}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Low dimensions (2D, 3D):} Dense causal structure $\to$ many links per element
    $\to$ high link action $\to$ penalized at moderate $\lambda$
  \item \textbf{High dimensions (5D+):} Sparse causal structure $\to$ maximum entropy $\to$
    dominates at low $\lambda$, but the entropy advantage is finite
  \item \textbf{4D:} Optimal balance between having enough causal structure to not be maximally
    penalized by the link action, while retaining enough entropy to not be defeated by
    lower dimensions
\end{itemize}

In more physical terms: within the present finite-ensemble setting, $3{+}1$ dimensions
are favored as the configuration where the causal light-cone structure is rich enough to impose
meaningful dynamical constraints, but not so dense as to over-restrict the available phase space.

\subsection{Relation to Carlip's Result}
\label{sec:carlip}

Carlip \cite{Carlip2024} argued that for the suppression of KR-like layered sets,
the link term in the causal-set action is the primary driver;
higher-order terms are needed to recover GR and locality but are not critical
for the suppression problem.

Our result extends this to the dimensional-selection problem among Lorentzian families:
within the present finite-ensemble setting,
the link term also governs dimensional selection,
and higher-order terms are again not needed (and are in fact counterproductive)
for this specific question.

The parallel suggests that link-density dynamics may play a more central role
in causal set theory than currently appreciated, at least for questions of structural selection.
Note that 5D is not a trivially weak competitor: separate pilot experiments
confirm that 5D wins $76\%$ of tested parameter cells under alternative scoring,
with no sign of performance saturation up to $N = 52$.
The 4D selection at $\lambda = 6{-}8$ therefore reflects genuine active suppression
of a strong 5D family.

\subsection{Status of $\Xi$ as a Physical Quantity}
\label{sec:xi_status}

The convergence of $\Xi_{4 \to 5}$ across three generators and across sizes up to $N = 112$
is striking. At the generator level, CV~$= 13.9\%$ over three geometries;
at the size level, Small-$N$ median $= 10.21$ vs Large-$N$ median $= 11.83$ (drift $15.8\%$).
We have evidence for a generator- and size-insensitive critical ratio,
not yet a proven universal constant. Several caveats apply:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Three generator types and seven sizes ($N = 20{-}112$) constitute suggestive
    but not exhaustive evidence. Curved-spacetime sprinkles, random causal triangulations,
    or sprinkles with non-trivial topology should be tested.
  \item The CV of $13.9\%$ (generator) and $17.7\%$ (size) are low but not negligible.
    The residual variation may reflect genuine $O(1)$ corrections due to
    generator-specific boundary effects, or may decrease with broader sampling.
    Regardless, the order-of-magnitude elevation of $\Xi_{4 \to 5}$ above
    lower-dimensional thresholds is robust and constitutes the core physical finding.
  \item The semi-analytic derivation (Section~\ref{sec:xi_derivation}) reproduces
    $\Xi_{4 \to 5} = 11.31$ from Minkowski geometry, using the ordering fraction
    $p_d$, the mean Alexandrov volume $\mathbb{E}[V_A]$, and the closure
    of Eq.~\eqref{eq:entropy_zero_param}. The residual error relative to the numerical
    median $11.35$ is only $0.4\%$. What remains open is the deeper microscopic
    explanation of why the entropy-transfer coefficient is so accurately captured
    by $\varepsilon = \pi/48$.
\end{itemize}

Despite these caveats, the stability of $\Xi_{4 \to 5}$ across three generators
and seven system sizes suggests it is a robust structural feature of
Minkowski-sprinkled causal sets, not an artifact of any particular generator or size regime.

We therefore characterize $\Xi_{4 \to 5}$ as \textbf{robust across all tested generators
and sizes}: stable across three generator geometries and seven system sizes,
with a geometrically motivated semi-analytic derivation now in hand,
but still awaiting broader numerical confirmation beyond the generator classes
studied here.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Finite size:} Our tested range now extends to $N = 112$.
    The 4D selection window shifts to higher $\lambda$ at $N \geq 96$ rather than
    persisting at $\lambda = 6{-}8$, but $\Xi_{4 \to 5}$ remains stable.
    Asymptotic $N \to \infty$ behavior cannot be guaranteed,
    though the link-density crossover analysis (Section~\ref{sec:link_crossover})
    provides a mechanistic explanation for the finite-size effects observed.

    Two observations support the relevance of the current size range.
    First, $\Xi_{4 \to 5}$ exhibits only $17.7\%$ variation across nearly two
    orders of magnitude in $N$ ($20$--$112$), and the semi-analytic
    derivation of Section~\ref{sec:xi_derivation} reproduces its value
    from Minkowski geometry alone, without reference to any particular system
    size.  This supports the interpretation that $\Xi$ captures a persistent geometric
    feature rather than a finite-size artifact.
    Second, the $\lambda$-window shift is itself well understood: it tracks
    the link-density crossover at $N \approx 96$ where 3D surpasses 4D
    in covering-relation count.  Crucially, the \emph{ratio} $\Xi_{4 \to 5}$
    does not drift with this crossover, indicating that the asymmetric
    barrier is encoded in the per-element geometry of the light cone,
    not in the total system size.

    \textbf{Asymptotic outlook.}
    The rightward window drift implies that, in the strict $N \to \infty$
    limit, the coupling $\lambda^*$ required for 4D selection grows.
    Whether $\lambda^*(N)$ diverges or saturates remains an open question.
    If the density crossover continues logarithmically, a finite $\lambda^*$
    may suffice; if it grows algebraically, asymptotic 4D selection would
    require coupling that scales with $N$.  Resolving this requires simulations
    at $N \gtrsim 500$, which are computationally demanding but feasible
    with modern SIS methods.  Regardless, the stability of $\Xi$ across
    the tested range suggests that the \emph{structural price} of the $4{\to}5$ transition
    may persist beyond the sizes studied here --- only the bookkeeping cost of maintaining 4D
    against 3D may change.

  \item \textbf{Sample size:} Each observable is computed from 4 samples per
    generator family per system size.  This yields stable mean estimates
    (coefficient of variation $\lesssim 18\%$ across sizes for $\Xi_{4\to5}$)
    but provides limited statistical power for distributional statements.
    Bootstrap confidence intervals on $\Xi$, slope, and median values would
    strengthen the quantitative conclusions and are planned for future work.

  \item \textbf{Coupling dependence:} The 4D window in $\lambda$-space shifts across
    generators (Section~\ref{sec:generator_robustness}).
    However, the dimensionless $\Xi$ analysis (Section~\ref{sec:xi_parameter}) absorbs
    this shift: the relevant physics is not which $\lambda$ wins,
    but that the intrinsic $4{\to}5$ penalty/entropy ratio is consistently $\sim 10$.

  \item \textbf{No continuum limit:} We use finite posets as structural probes,
    not as a controlled continuum limit.
    The relationship between finite competition winners and continuum physics remains indirect.
    This work addresses the \emph{structural selection} question --- which dimensionality
    is dynamically preferred in a discrete causal ensemble --- rather than the
    \emph{continuum-limit reconstruction} question of whether the selected structures
    converge to a smooth Lorentzian manifold as $N \to \infty$.
    The latter requires separate investigation of the scaling limit.

  \item \textbf{Generator scope:} While we have tested cube sprinkles and Alexandrov-set sprinkles,
    other Lorentzian-like constructions (e.g., random causal triangulations,
    curved-spacetime sprinkles) remain untested.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The central finding of this work is that, within finite Lorentzian-like causal-set
ensembles, the link action not only favors $3{+}1$ dimensions but does so for a
structurally identifiable reason.

The answer lies in the asymmetric structure of dimensional-boundary thresholds.
The dimensionless ratio $\Xi_{d \to d+1}$ --- measuring the link-penalty cost per unit of
entropy gained in crossing a dimensional boundary --- exhibits a clear hierarchy:
\begin{equation}
\Xi_{2 \to 3} \approx 2 \;\ll\; \Xi_{3 \to 4} \approx 3 \;\ll\; \Xi_{4 \to 5} \approx 10
\label{eq:xi_hierarchy}
\end{equation}
The $4{\to}5$ boundary carries an order-of-magnitude steeper penalty/entropy price than the
lower boundaries. This creates a one-sided barrier above 4D: at any coupling strong
enough to suppress 5D, 4D still out-competes 3D because the $3{\to}4$ barrier is low.
Four-dimensionality is therefore not a fine-tuned special case but a favored
finite-ensemble regime of the entropy--connectivity competition.

This asymmetric barrier is robust across tested conditions: $\Xi_{4 \to 5}$ converges to $\approx 10$ across
three independent generators (cube sprinkle, independent-seed cube, and Alexandrov-set diamond;
CV~$= 13.9\%$), even though the parameter-space location of the 4D selection window shifts
substantially between them. What the generators modulate is where the competition happens;
what they leave invariant is its intrinsic structural price.

Conversely, the standard BDG $d{=}4$ action, with literature coefficients designed for
continuum curvature recovery, systematically favors 5D: its higher-order interval corrections
reduce the effective $4{\to}5$ barrier below the entropy gradient, destroying dimensional selection.
This establishes a dual-layer picture: dimensional selection operates at the
\textbf{pre-geometric connectivity level} (links), while curvature reconstruction operates at the
\textbf{post-geometric continuum level} (full BDG). These are complementary but distinct.

In summary, the data support a picture in which
$3{+}1$ dimensions are favored by an asymmetric barrier in the link sector of
causal dynamics. $\Xi_{4 \to 5} \approx 11$ is stable from $N = 20$ to $N = 112$
(CV~$= 17.7\%$) and across three generator geometries (CV~$= 13.9\%$).
The continuum-limit implications of this finite-ensemble result remain
an open question for future work.

\section{Scaling-Law Fitting Details}
\label{app:fitting_details}

This appendix documents the fitting procedure underlying the analytical derivation
of $\Xi_{4 \to 5}$ (Section~\ref{sec:xi_derivation}).

\paragraph{Data.}
Observables $C_0/N$ and $H/N$ are computed for $d = 2, 3, 4, 5$ at seven system sizes
$N \in \{20, 36, 52, 68, 80, 96, 112\}$ using the cube-sprinkle generator (4 samples per cell,
SIS entropy with 4096 runs for $N > N_\text{exact}$).

\paragraph{Link-density fit.}
For each dimension $d$, we fit $C_0/N = a_d \cdot N^{\alpha_d}$ by ordinary least squares
on the log-transformed data $\log(C_0/N) = \log a_d + \alpha_d \cdot \log N$.
All fits achieve $R^2 > 0.95$, with $d = 4$ and $d = 5$ exceeding $0.98$.

\paragraph{Entropy-density fit.}
For each $d$, $H/N = b_d \cdot \ln N + c_d$ is fitted by ordinary least squares.
All fits achieve $R^2 > 0.98$, confirming the log-linear law across the full $N$ range.

\paragraph{Parameter extrapolation.}
The four parameter sequences $(a_d, \alpha_d, b_d, c_d)$ are modeled as smooth functions of $d$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $a_d = A \cdot e^{-\kappa d}$ (exponential decay, $A = 2.655$, $\kappa = 0.760$)
  \item $\alpha_d = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 d$ (linear, $\alpha_0 = -0.069$, $\alpha_1 = 0.225$)
  \item $b_d = b_\infty - B e^{-\mu d}$ (saturating exponential, $b_\infty = 0.877$, $B = 0.977$, $\mu = 0.457$)
  \item $c_d$: quadratic fit in $d$
\end{itemize}
These models are fitted to $d = 2{-}5$ and extrapolated to $d = 6, 7$ to produce the blind
predictions in Section~\ref{sec:blind_predictions}.

\paragraph{Sensitivity.}
The closed-form $\Xi_{4 \to 5}(N)$ is evaluated at multiple reference sizes.
It varies by less than $11.4\%$ across $N = 36{-}112$, confirming that the prediction is
insensitive to the choice of $N_\text{ref}$.
The systematic $\sim 27\%$ overestimation of $\Xi_{5 \to 6}$ and $\Xi_{6 \to 7}$
(Section~\ref{sec:numerical_verification}) is attributable to the exponential model for $a_d$
decaying too aggressively beyond its training domain ($d = 2{-}5$).
